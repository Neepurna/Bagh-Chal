History of Baghchal

The moving tigers of Nepal

Ancient Origins

Bagh-Chal translates to “Tigers' Moves” or “The Moving Tigers” in Nepali. It is an ancient asymmetric strategy board game from Nepal, widely regarded as the country's unofficial national game.

Believed to be over a thousand years old, the game reflects the delicate relationship between Himalayan herders, their livestock, and the apex predators that shared the same ecological world.

Cultural and Ecological Roots

Tigers were historically present in the lower foothills and dense jungles of Nepal's Terai region. As settlements and grazing lands expanded, predators frequently came into conflict with village cattle and goat herds.

Oral traditions say shepherds and cattle herders created Bagh-Chal during long grazing hours. Early boards were scratched into dirt or stone, with small pebbles as bakhri, the goats, and larger stones as bagh, the tigers.

Myth and Alternate Names

Although its practical origin belongs to pastoral life, Nepali mythology adds another layer. Local folklore links the creation of Bagh-Chal to Mandodari, a figure connected with the sacred Panchakanya maidens in Hindu tradition.

In Nepali, the game is known as Bagh Chal. Among the indigenous Newar community of the Kathmandu Valley, it is traditionally called Dhun Kasa.

Hunt Game Lineage

In historical ludology, Bagh-Chal belongs to the family of Hunt Games or Tiger Games: strategy games where a few powerful hunters face a larger group of weaker pieces.

The 5 by 5 board, with 25 intersections connected by orthogonal and diagonal lines, shares geometry with Alquerque, an ancient Middle Eastern strategy game and a precursor to modern draughts. Bagh-Chal also shares mechanical DNA with Indian hunt games such as Aadu Puli Attam, the Lambs and Tigers Game.

Preservation and Modern Revival

For generations, Bagh-Chal survived through imitation, visual observation, and oral teaching in village paatis, public resting porches, and family spaces.

Urbanization and mobile technology reduced physical play among younger generations, raising concern that the game could fade from everyday life despite its deep cultural footprint.

Today, a revival is taking shape through artisan brass boards, international strategy tournaments, and digital versions such as BaghChal.net. Online play, advanced bots, and multiplayer systems help carry the game beyond Nepal while preserving its original strategic soul.
